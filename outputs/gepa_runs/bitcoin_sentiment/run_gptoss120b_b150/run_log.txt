   - Discard any sentence that only reports past prices, past events, or historical data (e.g., “Bitcoin traded at $90k yesterday”, “last month it fell 20%”).

2. **Classify each forward‑looking statement**  
   - **Bullish cue**: predicts higher price, upside movement, accumulation, reduced sell pressure, supportive macro or regulatory environment, technical breakout to the upside, overbought → bullish reversal, etc.  
   - **Bearish cue**: predicts lower price, downside movement, distribution, increased sell pressure, negative macro/regulatory impact, technical breakdown, oversold → further decline, etc.  
   - **Neutral cue**: states uncertainty, gives a range that includes both up and down, says “could go either way”, “uncertain”, “mixed”, “no clear direction”, or merely describes a factor without a clear bias.

3. **Weight the cues by strength of language**  
   - **Strong language** (e.g., “will reach”, “is set to break”, “strongly expect”, “definitely”, “significant”, “major”, “record”, “breakout confirmed”, “collapse”, “crash”) → **weight = +2** for bullish or **‑2** for bearish.  
   - **Moderate language** (e.g., “likely”, “expected to”, “could”, “might”, “possible”, “potential”, “could see”, “may experience”) → **weight = +1** for bullish or **‑1** for bearish.  
   - **Weak/hedged language** (e.g., “might be”, “could be”, “if… then”, “some analysts say”) → **weight = 0** (treated as neutral for scoring purposes).

4. **Compute a net sentiment score (N)**  
   - Sum the weights of all bullish cues (**B**) and all bearish cues (**R**):  
     `N = Σ(weight of bullish cues)  –  Σ(weight of bearish cues)`  
   - Neutral cues do not affect the sum.

5. **Map the net sentiment N to the 1‑15 scale**  

| Net sentiment N | Resulting score (choose the middle of the indicated sub‑range) |
|-----------------|--------------------------------------------------------------|
| N ≤ ‑5          | 1 – 2  (strongly bearish)                                    |

| N = +3          | 14     (bullish)                                             |
| N ≥ +4          | 15     (strongly bullish)                                    |

   - When N falls exactly on a boundary (e.g., N = ‑2), pick the lower value of the sub‑range to avoid over‑rating.  
   - If the article contains **both bullish and bearish cues of comparable strength** (N close to 0), the score will land in the neutral band (6‑10).

### Common pitfalls to avoid
- **Do not count past price statements** as forward‑looking cues.  
- **Do not let a single strong bullish quote dominate** if the rest of the article presents several bearish or neutral expectations; the weighting system ensures balance.  
- **When the article lists a price range** (e.g., “$89k‑$108k”) treat it as neutral unless the surrounding commentary clearly favours one side of the range.  
- **If the article only repeats expert opinions without a clear consensus**, the net N will be near 0 → neutral score (6‑10).  
- **Avoid “off‑by‑one” errors**: verify that the chosen integer falls in the sub‑range indicated by N.

| **Price‑direction verbs**: rise, fall, increase, decline, break, bounce, rally, dip, drop, recover, surge, plunge, etc. | Directional |
| **Time markers**: “next week/month/quarter/year”, “by Q4”, “in September 2025”, “over the next 4‑6 weeks”, “later this year”, etc. | Future |
| **Forecast language**: “could”, “might”, “may”, “potentially”, “likely”, “expected to”, “should”, “will”, “is set to”, “is poised to”, “is expected to”, “is likely to”, “is projected to”, “forecast”, “prediction”, “target”, “price target”, “price range”, “scenario”, “outlook”, “scenario”, “range‑bound”, “breakout”, “bull case”, “bear case”, “bullish”, “bearish”, “neutral”. | Forecast |
| **Analyst / consensus statements**: “analyst says”, “experts believe”, “most analysts think”, “majority view”, “consensus”, “survey shows”, etc. | Forward‑looking opinion |

**Exclude** any sentence that:

* Only reports past price levels, historical percentages, or events that have already happened (e.g., “Bitcoin fell 6 % yesterday”, “last month it was $120k”).  
* Describes a current technical indicator *without* any implied future price direction (e.g., “RSI = 57 – neutral”).  
* Is purely explanatory (e.g., “The halving reduces supply”) unless it is explicitly tied to a future price impact (“the next halving is expected to trigger a rally”).

If the **title** itself contains a forward‑looking claim (e.g., “Bitcoin could hit $200K in 2025”), treat it as a separate forward‑looking sentence.

---

## 2. Determine the **direction** of each forward‑looking sentence  

| Direction | Typical wording |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Bullish** | Predicts a rise, higher price, upside, breakout, new ATH, “will go higher”, “expected to rally”, “bull case”, “bullish”. |
| **Bearish** | Predicts a fall, lower price, downside, correction, “will drop”, “expected to fall”, “bear case”, “bearish”. |
| **Neutral / Undetermined** | States an event or range *without* a clear price direction (e.g., “could stay range‑bound between $89k‑$108k”, “may trade sideways”). Treat as **neutral** unless the range is skewed heavily toward the lower bound (then count as weakly bearish) or the upper bound (weakly bullish).  

If a sentence contains **both** bullish and bearish cues, **split the weight proportionally** (e.g., “could rise but may also correct” → 1.5 bullish, 1.5 bearish).

---

## 3. Assign a **certainty weight** to each directional cue  

| Certainty level | Weight | Typical cue words/phrases |
|-----------------|--------|---------------------------|
| **Weak (1)** | 1 | could, might, may, possibly, potentially, “likely (but not certain)”, seems to, appears to, “suggests”, “could be”. |
| **Moderate (2)** | 2 | likely, expected to, probable, tends to, generally, usually, “is expected to”, “should”, “is projected to”, “forecast”, “price target”. |
| **Strong (3)** | 3 | will, is set to, is poised to, definitely, guaranteed, “strongly”, “clearly”, “is expected to (with high confidence)”, “is forecast to”. |

*If a sentence contains multiple cue words, use the **strongest** cue present.*  
*If a sentence is a quoted expert opinion (e.g., “Analyst X says Bitcoin will rise”), treat the certainty as **moderate (2)** unless the quote itself contains a strong cue.*

---

## 4. Calculate **weighted totals**  

For every forward‑looking sentence:

1. Determine its **directional weight** (bullish or bearish) using the certainty weight from step 3.  
2. **Add** that weight to the appropriate accumulator:  

* **B** = sum of all bullish weights.  
* **R** = sum of all bearish weights.  

Neutral statements do **not** contribute to B or R, but they are kept in mind for the final “neutral‑band” adjustment (see step 6).

---

## 5. Determine the **overall direction**  

| Condition | Result |
|-----------|--------|
| **B > R** | Overall **bullish** |
| **R > B** | Overall **bearish** |
| **|B − R| ≤ 1** | **Neutral** (even if B and R are non‑zero) |

---

## 6. Map to the **1‑to‑15 score**  

### 6.1 Compute the **dominance ratio**  

```
D = (DominantWeight − OppositeWeight) / (DominantWeight + OppositeWeight)
```

*DominantWeight* = max(B, R)  
*OppositeWeight* = min(B, R)  

`D` ranges from 0 (perfect balance) to 1 (all weight on one side).

### 6.2 Select the **base score**  

| Direction | D range | Base score |
|-----------|---------|------------|
| **Bearish** | 0.00 – 0.20 | 2 |
|           | 0.21 – 0.40 | 3 |
|           | 0.41 – 0.60 | 4 |
|           | 0.61 – 1.00 | 5 |
| **Neutral** | any (when B≈R) | 8 (middle of neutral band) |
| **Bullish** | 0.00 – 0.20 | 11 |
|           | 0.21 – 0.40 | 12 |
|           | 0.41 – 0.60 | 13 |
|           | 0.61 – 1.00 | 14‑15* |

\* **14‑15 rule:**  
* If the **dominant side** contains **any strong‑certainty (weight = 3) statements**, give **15**.  
* Otherwise give **14**.

### 6.3 Neutral‑band fine‑tuning  

*If the article contains **explicit language** that leans weakly bullish (e.g., “most analysts think price may rise”) **or** weakly bearish (e.g., “most analysts expect a dip”), shift the neutral score one step toward that side (11 for a weak bullish nudge, 5 for a weak bearish nudge).  
*If the article has **no forward‑looking statements at all**, default to **8**.
